The IMF projects Canada to post the second-fastest growth in the G7 over 2026–2027. However, the Bank of Canada notes GDP contracted 0.6% in Q4 2025, and the economy is currently operating with excess supply, weighing on the near-term outlook.

Labour Market

Employment Trends

Unemployment rate (%) — 2024 to 2026
National rate Youth rate (15–24)
National unemployment peaked at 7.1% in Sept 2025, fell to 6.7% by March 2026. Youth unemployment above 14%.
💼

Job creation outpacing the US

Since early 2025, Canada added nearly 3x as many jobs per capita (3.4 per 1,000) as the United States (1.2 per 1,000), with the majority in the private sector.

⚠️

Youth unemployment a concern

The 15–24 age cohort saw employment fall by 64,000 in Jan–Feb 2026 alone, pushing youth unemployment back above 14% — the highest in years outside the pandemic.

📉

Immigration slowdown biting

Federal pullback on non-permanent residents and international students is shrinking the labour force, projected to be ~0.2% smaller by end-2026, dampening both hiring and demand.

The average US tariff rate on Canadian goods stands at 6–7% — below the 17% applied to many other nations. While most CUSMA-compliant goods are exempt from reciprocal tariffs, key industries like autos (up to 25%), steel, aluminum, and forestry products continue to face elevated duties. Canada has run a current account deficit for 17 consecutive years.

🏘️

Ontario & BC softening most

National average home prices are expected to be flat to slightly lower in 2026, with Ontario and BC seeing the largest corrections due to reduced investor activity and high valuations.

📈

Montreal holds stronger

Quebec's housing market — particularly Montreal and Quebec City — is showing more resilience than the national average, supported by stable buyer sentiment and more moderate price levels.

🔨

Starts at risk of declining

The Canadian Home Builders Association's Housing Market Index sank to record lows in Q3 2025. Residential construction (~8% of GDP) faces headwinds from high costs and tariff-driven material price pressures.

Analysis

Key Risks & Opportunities

🇺🇸

US trade uncertainty

Ongoing tariffs on autos, steel, aluminum and forestry are depressing exports and business investment. CUSMA renewal review by July 2026 adds further policy uncertainty to an already fragile outlook.

🛢️

Middle East oil shock

The regional conflict has pushed WTI to ~US$80/barrel, inflating gasoline prices and squeezing consumers. Core CPI is being pushed higher, limiting room for further BoC rate cuts in 2026.

📊

Productivity gap

Canada's GDP per capita is above the OECD average but below top performers, with a structural productivity deficit that has widened in recent years. Capital stock per worker remains below the G7 average.

🏛️

Fiscal deficit expansion

Budget deficit ballooned to ~$80B (2.5% of GDP) as the government responds to trade shocks with stimulus, defence spending increases, and sector support. Fiscal space is narrowing as debt servicing costs rise.

🌍

Leading G7 in FDI inflows

Canada continues to attract the most per-capita foreign direct investment in the G7, reflecting confidence in its institutions, legal framework, and resource base despite near-term headwinds.

💰

Real wages recovering

Hourly wages are rising modestly above inflation for the first time since 2020, providing a small but meaningful boost to household purchasing power and consumer confidence heading into H2 2026.
